Breakthroughs in Biology from a Bioinformatic Perspective

About the Workshop Part I

Recombinant technology is one of the most important features of modern biology. Starting from the early 1970s, this technique has been used almost everywhere, from creating genetically modified crops that can resist extreme conditions, to cheaply manufacturing insulin at large quantities (as opposed to sourcing it from pig's pancreas!) to treat millions of diabetes patients. Some of the most fascinating and important recent discoveries in medicine and genetics have been brought about by these crucial ideas and is something that any essential budding researcher must know. Even people who are not actively involved in this field are definitely affected by it, and even at a cursory glance, will learn more about their bodies work. Thus, it has cemented itself as not just an integral part of biology, but a justifiable research field of its own. Recombinant technology forms the base of a large number of genetic engineering projects. It's essentially the idea of the artificially transferring of genes from one organism to another host cells.

Part II

Have you ever wondered why your body works as efficiently as it does? There is amazing coordination between bodily requirement and fulfilment, and that delicate balance is maintained by neatly organised events.
The technical rundown: The visual translation of the genetic code in all our cells is represented by proteins, the building blocks of life. Proteins are destined for export to target regions, intercellular trafficking and organelle biogenesis. The essential need to understand their generation and further complexity lead to the formulation of the theory of signal hypothesis by Blobel and Sabitini in 1971.
The proposed theory discusses the whereabouts of different proteins and the various routes they take to reach their destination. A key factor responsible for the intricate pathway is the signal sequence, this is a gene sequence for a protein that assigns a target site (usually a cellular membrane) for the protein to get attached to. Base pair mutations in this gene sequence can lead to terrible altercations leading to a breakdown of the trafficking process. Genetic techniques of finding out the mutation require biotechnology tools, and these will be discussed thoroughly, as well as what the point of all of this is, in this workshop.
All this will be understood via hands-on bioinformatic analysis and thorough discussion. Bioinformatics is a field at the intersection between biology and statistics and leverages a number of computer software programs in practice. It is not only very captivating but also very useful for carrying out general biological research. Bioinformatic tools used would help in visualisation of proteins, structural comparison of proteins and gene sequence comparisons.

Target Audience

This workshop is for students interested in genetics, biology or research in general. You do not need a high level of base knowledge, although reading through the resources is highly suggested.
